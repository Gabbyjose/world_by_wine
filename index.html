Loire Valley

This valley is known for its earthy and light Cabernet Francs.

Alsace

Once a German region, now French, check out their Reislings and try to taste the difference.

Bordeaux

A French classic, who hasn't had a Bordeaux? Made up of primarily Cabernet Sauvnignon, these wines are blends.

Rhone Valley

Hermitage hermitage hermitage!

Burgundy

This is the home of Pinot Noirs.

Mendoza

People come here for malbecs but you can also find cabernet sauvignons, pinot noirs, cabernet francs.

Salta

Thought malbecs only came from Mendoza? Wrong. Check out this malbec haven.

Ribera Del Duero

Just your typical tempranillo grape but with years of oak aging that leaves you with a remarkable, smokey finish.

Rioja

Tempranillo, Granache, oh my! Spanish grapes are abundant and unique here.

Catalunya

Barcelona isn't the only bustling part of this area. Check out these lesser known wineries with grape names you can't even pronounce.

Galicia

Even more remote, this north western part of the country produces some of the best wines.

Napa Valley, Sonoma Valley

Who hasn't heard of California wines? Some call it the new Bordeaux, so try a Cabernet Sauvignon and evaluate for yourself how this compares to its French counterpart.

Finger Lakes, and Hamptoms

Some call this the big apple, others call this the new home for Cabernet Franc. Drive up north to the Finger Lakes and discover something new.

Willa-who?

Want a Pinot Noir but don't want to spend a lot of money? Go to Washington. Specifically, go to Willamette valley.

The Western Cape

Shiraz. Enough said.
